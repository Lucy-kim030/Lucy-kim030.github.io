About Me

Where Engineering Meets Human Experience

I'm a PhD candidate at Monash University's Department of Electrical and Computer Systems Engineering, affiliated with the Biomedicine Discovery Institute. My research sits at the intersection of embedded systems, human-computer interaction, and assistive technology — spanning wearable multimodal interfaces for non-visual guidance and simulated prosthetic vision with haptic feedback.

I've always been fascinated by human vision — how it shapes our cognition, emotions, and everyday actions. My interest in cameras and optics naturally drew me to understanding sight itself. A chance encounter with a visually impaired student at university, and watching their YouTube videos, revealed how profoundly the loss of vision disrupts actions we take for granted. That moment ignited my passion for assistive technology — from wearable haptic guidance systems to the frontier of prosthetic vision and brain-computer interfaces. At the core, I want to understand how touch and other senses can expand to fill the role of sight.

What sets my work apart is the rare combination of low-level hardware skills (FPGA design, circuit fabrication, sensor integration) with rigorous human-centered research methods. I don't just build the device — I design the experiments, run the user studies, and analyze how humans interact with the technology I create.

Outside the lab, I'm someone who loves making things with my hands — whether that's cooking, drawing, or building circuits. I'm drawn to sports (anything with a ball, plus running), and I find peace in watching how nature unfolds through the lens of my Fujifilm camera. I'm a fan of sci-fi and animation that pushes beyond what we can imagine, and I'm always looking to challenge myself and explore new areas.

Research

Publications & Projects

My research explores how spatial design of multimodal feedback shapes non-visual interaction — with direct applications in wearable assistive technology.

Submitted to Journal

Spatial Alignment of Audio–Haptic Directional Feedback Shapes Non-Visual Hand Guidance

International Journal of Human-Computer Studies (IJHCS) — Under Review

Cue alignment matters more than modality count. In a controlled experiment with 20 participants across 4 feedback conditions, we demonstrated that aligned dual-cue feedback (audio + haptic, spatially congruent) achieved the best path efficiency in non-visual reaching tasks. Simply adding more feedback modalities doesn't help — how they are spatially mapped is what matters. Direct implications for wearable assistive device design.

Multimodal Feedback Spatial Alignment Non-Visual Guidance User Study (N=20) Path Efficiency
Conference Poster

Spatial Alignment of Multimodal Feedback for Non-Visual Hand Guidance

Society for Neuroscience (SfN) 2025 & IEEE EMBS 2025 — Poster Presentations

Presented findings on how spatial congruency between audio and haptic cues affects hand guidance performance in non-visual reaching tasks. Demonstrated the critical role of feedback alignment in multimodal assistive interface design.

Evaluating Wrist-Worn Vibrotactile Feedback for Directional Guidance in Users with Visual Impairment

APEC 2024 (Poster) • EPSM 2024 (Oral Presentation)

Evaluated the effectiveness of wrist-worn vibrotactile feedback systems for providing directional guidance. Presented initial findings on how vibration patterns can encode spatial direction information for users with visual impairments.
